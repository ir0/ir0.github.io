1st Colloquium on AI4AEC
Is AI ready for the building industry?
(and vice-versa)

About

The past few years a lot of discussion has been sparked on AI in the Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) industry. Despite advancements in this interdisciplinary field, we still have not answered fundamental questions about adopting and adapting AI technology for the AEC industry. In this 1st Colloquium on AI4AEC, we would like to explore if AI is ready for the building industry and focus on 3 main questions: what can we do using off-the-shelf AI technology in practice , what we’ll be able to do in the foreseeable future, and what problems we don’t know how to solve.

In two half-day sessions on August 19th and 20th 2020 that will take place online, we will examine 6 AEC topics. Each topic will be covered in an integrative manner by a team of industry and academia to overcome siloed limits of understanding. Presentations will be followed by hands-on sessions where participants and presenters will be called to further explore what was discussed with practical activities. We will conclude the colloquium with a "reflections" session on August 26th.

One of our main goals is to identify the most promising AEC areas for applying AI, in which priority we should be focusing our attention and why. From the above, the industry professionals will learn about the leading and bleeding edge of AI, including a comparison of what people can do vs machines. The academic audience will learn about how industry is already using AI, what they see on the near-term horizon, and what they would like to do but don’t have a solution in sight.

Structural Engineering

AI in Structural Engineering Today



Abstract

We present an overview of AI and optimization in the context of structural design. The first section describes the integration between existing Topology Optimization methodologies and high-rise building design and component manufacturing. We proceed to illustrate current CIFE research efforts to automate the design of structural components of commercial steel building structures for improved speed, cost, design time, and safety. We conclude the presentation with automated crane operation methods and the potential integration of this technology with structural design AI.

Keynote Talk

How can Computer Vison and Mixed Reality help Architecture, Engineering and Construction?



Abstract

Geometric computer vision has found many applications in recent years. Highly relevant for AEC are photogrammetry and 3D reconstruction to help with surveying or building digital twins. These 3D models have evolved from purely geometric to include semantic understanding. Simultaneous localization and mapping, so called SLAM, often builds on 3D computer vision techniques to enable mixed reality experiences, but also robotic navigation which are all starting to find applications in AEC. To help users navigate inside a facility, or in the wider world, image-based localization techniques are used to build the AR cloud. In this talk we’ll cover these topics and discuss some of the challenges and limitations.

His main area of research is computer vision. One of his main research goals is to develop flexible approaches to capture visual representations of real world objects, scenes and events. Fast Prototyping with VR/AR

Abstract

The AEC industry, being rife with risk around cost and schedule, presents itself as a huge beneficiary of both virtual and augmented reality HMD technology. Being able to simulate designs or practice complex logistics virtually reduces costly mistakes and safety issues. But why aren’t job sites and architectural studios rife with users walking around with these HMDs? While highly beneficial to projects, VR and AR HMDs still manifest critical user experience issues that are hindering ubiquitous use of the technology and require complex engineering to overcome.

Current VR and AR hardware potential limits for the adoption of such systems in the construction industry and beyond will be discussed. Recent efforts to improve the realism, comfort, and accessibility of VR and AR systems will be presented.

For VR to be successful it needs to be integrated in the collaboration ecosystem of any project team and organization. It needs to be embedded in the daily work practice and process. Examples from the AEC Global Teamwork will demonstrate how we reimagine collaboration in distributed project teamwork and highlight transformative impacts of VR immersive experiences at individual and team level, and on product and process.

As-Is Information

It's All About Trust.



Abstract

Effective construction progress management enables accurate resource allocation on a project, which in turn drives design, cost, schedule, and quality on projects. Due to the absence of (automated) data-driven analysis, industry experts currently rely on their knowledge and experience to make resource allocation decisions. Recently, AI tools that support faster decision making have appeared and, although, speed is important to maintain a project informed, trustworthy decisions are preferred. While AI can accelerate conclusions from complex data, industry experts need to be able to trust their decisions. This discussion will explore the current workflow for progress management while also considering the impact of AI within the context of trust and certainty.

Keynote Talk

A Human-Centered Perspective on Collaboration with Robots



Abstract

Today’s society is rapidly advancing towards robotics systems that interact and collaborate with humans, e.g., semi-autonomous vehicles interacting with drivers and pedestrians, medical robots used in collaboration with doctors, or service robots interacting with their users in smart homes. Formalizing interaction is a crucial component in seamless collaboration and coordination between humans and today's robotics systems. In this talk, I will discuss a journey in formalizing human-robot interaction, and how that can help advance the intersection of robotics and the building industry. Specifically, I will first discuss developing data-efficient techniques to learn computational models of human behavior. I will continue with the challenges that arise when agents (including humans and robots) interact with each other. Further, I will argue that in many applications, a full computational human model is not necessary for seamless and efficient interaction. Instead, in many collaborative tasks, conventions —low-dimensional shared representations of tasks — is sufficient for capturing the interaction between agents. I will conclude the talk with challenges around adapting conventions in human-robot applications such as assistive teleoperation and collaborative transport.
